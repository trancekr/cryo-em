Electron Microscopy & Biological Samples
Why do we need cryo-EM?

The Problem

  • Vacuum damage — Water evaporates instantly in the EM column vacuum, destroying hydrated biological structure
  • Radiation damage — High-energy electrons break chemical bonds; biological specimens are extremely radiation-sensitive
  • Fixation artifacts — Chemical fixation and dehydration alter native protein conformation
  • Low contrast — Light atoms (C, N, O) scatter electrons weakly, producing very low signal-to-noise images
  • Charging — Non-conductive biological specimens accumulate charge under electron beam
  • Thickness limit — Specimen must be <100 nm for electron transmission
→

The Cryo Solution

Flash-freeze the specimen in vitreous (amorphous) ice to preserve its native, hydrated state — no fixation, no staining, no dehydration.

  • Vitreous ice — Rapid cooling (~104 °C/s) in liquid ethane prevents crystalline ice formation
  • Native state — Proteins remain in solution-like conformation within the ice layer
  • Reduced damage — Imaging at liquid nitrogen temperature (−196 °C) slows radiation damage
  • Low-dose imaging — Only 20–60 e−/Å² total dose; compensated by averaging thousands of particle images
Dubochet et al., Q Rev Biophys (1988) · Glaeser, Biophys J (1971) · Taylor & Glaeser, Science (1974)
Conventional TEM vs Cryo-EM
Two approaches to electron microscopy of biological specimens

Conventional TEM

  • Preparation: Chemical fixation, dehydration, heavy metal staining (uranyl acetate, phosphotungstic acid)
  • Advantages: High contrast, simple prep, radiation resistant samples
  • Limitations: Artifacts from staining/dehydration; limited resolution (~15–20 Å); only surface topology
  • Best for: Quick screening, morphology assessment, quality control
vs

Cryo-EM

  • Preparation: Flash-freezing in vitreous ice — no fixation, no staining, no dehydration
  • Advantages: Native state preservation, near-atomic resolution (≤2 Å), multiple conformations
  • Limitations: Low SNR, radiation sensitivity, expensive equipment, complex sample prep
  • Best for: High-resolution structures, membrane proteins, large complexes
Henderson, Q Rev Biophys (1995) · Glaeser, Nat Methods (2016)
Key Components of Modern Cryo-TEM
Zero-loss filtering: prism + energy slit
Indirect (scintillator+CCD) vs Direct (CMOS)
DQE: Direct detector > Film > CCD
Cryo Holder / Autoloader
  • Maintains specimen at −180 °C (liquid nitrogen)
  • Autoloader cassette holds up to 12 grids
  • Preserves vacuum during grid exchange
  • Enables unattended multi-grid data collection
Energy Filter (Zero-loss)
  • Prism separates electrons by energy loss
  • Energy slit selects only zero-loss (elastic) electrons
  • Removes inelastic scattering → improves contrast & SNR
  • Essential for thick specimens (>50 nm)
Direct Electron Detector (DED)
  • No scintillator → direct electron counting on CMOS
  • DQE >2× over film/CCD at all spatial frequencies
  • Single electron counting mode maximizes SNR
  • Movie-mode enables motion correction
Images: MyScope (myscope.training), Microscopy Australia · McMullan et al., Ultramicroscopy (2009) · Faruqi & McMullan, Q Rev Biophys (2011)

Typical Project Timeline

  • Protein purification: 2–3 months (expression, purification, SEC/DLS/Mass Phot. quality assessment)
  • Sample screening: ~2 weeks (negative stain + initial cryo-EM grid optimization)
  • Data collection: 1–3 days per dataset
  • Processing: Hours to days (GPU-intensive)
  • Model building: Days to weeks
  • Total: ~1–6 months from purified protein to deposited structure

Critical Bottleneck

Sample preparation is the most time-consuming and unpredictable step. Grid optimization alone can take weeks of trial-and-error — ice thickness, particle distribution, and orientation are all highly variable.

Tip: Invest in thorough biochemical characterization (SEC, DLS, mass photometry) before moving to cryo-EM grid preparation. Poor sample quality is the #1 cause of failed projects.

Cheng et al., Cell (2015) · Thompson et al., Star Protocols (2021) · Passmore & Russo, Methods Enzymol (2016)
Sample Preparation — Overview & Challenges

Key Challenges

  • Size limitation — Particles below ~50 kDa have low SNR; may require scaffold or Fab augmentation
  • Sample purity — Requires highly purified, monodisperse protein (>90% homogeneity)
  • Concentration — 0.5–5 mg/mL; too high = aggregation, too low = empty holes
  • Vitrification — Ice thickness, preferred orientation, air-water interface denaturation
  • Reproducibility — Manual blotting remains variable; automated systems improving

Key Requirement

The specimen must be biochemically well-behaved: stable, monodisperse, and structurally homogeneous in the buffer conditions used for grid preparation.

Halfon et al., Faraday Discuss (2022) · Weissenberger et al., Nat Methods (2021) · Drulyte et al., Acta Cryst D (2018)
Problems and Limitations of Cryo-EM

Sample

  • Size limitation — Particles <50 kDa challenging (low SNR)
  • Purity required — >90% homogeneity essential
  • Vitrification — Ice thickness, preferred orientation, AWI denaturation
  • Reproducibility — Manual blotting highly variable

Microscopy

  • Beam damage — Electrons destroy biological structure
  • Low dose — Only 20–60 e−/Å² allowed → very noisy images
  • Imperfect imaging — CTF, lens aberrations, specimen charging
  • Equipment cost — Titan Krios: $5–8M + $500K/yr maintenance

Data Processing

  • Complex pipeline — Motion correction → CTF → picking → 2D/3D
  • Heterogeneity — Multiple conformations complicate analysis
  • Interpretation — Map quality varies; model bias risk
  • Computing — GPU clusters needed; TB-scale data

The Bottom Line

The process remains slow, manual, and non-reproducible — especially sample preparation. Despite the “resolution revolution,” cryo-EM still requires significant expertise at every step. Sample quality is the #1 determinant of success.

Glaeser, Nat Methods (2016) · Drulyte et al., Acta Cryst D (2018) · Henderson, Angew Chem (2013)
