Section 1: Cryo-EM Overview
Electron Microscopy & Biological Samples
Why do we need cryo-EM?
The Problem
  • Vacuum damage — Water evaporates instantly in EM column vacuum
  • Radiation damage — High-energy electrons break chemical bonds
  • Fixation artifacts — Chemical fixation alters native conformation
  • Low contrast — Light atoms scatter electrons weakly
  • Charging — Non-conductive specimens accumulate charge
The Cryo Solution
  • Vitreous ice — Rapid cooling (~10⁴ C/s) in liquid ethane
  • Native state — Proteins remain in solution-like conformation
  • Reduced damage — Imaging at -196 C slows radiation damage
  • Low-dose imaging — 20-60 e-/A2; compensated by averaging thousands
  • No staining — Flash-freeze preserves native hydrated state
Dubochet et al., Q Rev Biophys (1988) · Glaeser, J Ultrastruct Res (1971) · Taylor & Glaeser, Science (1974)
Section 1: Cryo-EM Overview
Conventional TEM vs Cryo-EM
Two approaches to electron microscopy of biological specimens
Conventional TEM
  • Preparation: Chemical fixation, dehydration, heavy metal staining
  • Advantages: High contrast, simple prep, radiation resistant
  • Limitations: Artifacts; limited resolution (~15-20 A); surface only
  • Best for: Quick screening, morphology assessment
Cryo-EM
  • Preparation: Flash-freezing in vitreous ice — no fixation/staining
  • Advantages: Native state, near-atomic resolution (≤2 A)
  • Limitations: Low SNR, radiation sensitivity, expensive equipment
  • Best for: High-resolution structures, membrane proteins

Section 1: Cryo-EM Overview
Key Components of Modern Cryo-TEM
Cryo Holder / Autoloader
  • Maintains specimen at -180 C (liquid nitrogen)
  • Autoloader cassette holds up to 12 grids
  • Preserves vacuum during grid exchange
  • Enables unattended multi-grid collection
Energy Filter (Zero-loss)
  • Prism separates electrons by energy loss
  • Energy slit selects zero-loss electrons
  • Removes inelastic scattering — improves SNR
  • Essential for thick specimens (>50 nm)
Direct Electron Detector
  • No scintillator — direct electron counting on CMOS
  • DQE >2x over film/CCD at all frequencies
  • Single electron counting mode maximizes SNR
  • Movie-mode enables motion correction

Section 1: Cryo-EM Overview
SPA Workflow — Overview
Four stages from sample to deposited structure
1. Sample Preparation
  • Purification: Express, purify, assess monodispersity
  • Negative stain: Quick TEM check for particle quality
  • Grid prep: Apply sample, blot, plunge-freeze
  • Screening: Evaluate ice, particle distribution, orientation
Weeks to months
2. Data Collection
  • Grid loading: Load grid, acquire low-mag atlas
  • Area selection: Find thin vitreous ice regions
  • Acquisition: EPU/SerialEM (search-focus-record)
  • Movies: 1,000-10,000+ dose-fractionated frames
1-3 days per dataset
3. Image Processing
  • Motion correction: Align frames, dose weighting
  • CTF estimation: Determine & correct defocus
  • Picking & 2D: Extract, sort, remove junk particles
  • 3D reconstruction: Orientation assignment, 3D map
  • Validation: Gold-standard FSC (0.143 threshold)
Hours to days (GPU)
4. Model Building
  • Map fitting: Fit coordinates into density (Coot, ISOLDE)
  • Refinement: Real-space refinement (Phenix)
  • Validation: FSC, MolProbity, map-model correlation
  • Deposition: Submit to PDB and EMDB
Days to weeks

Section 1: Cryo-EM Overview
Problems and Limitations of Cryo-EM
Three major challenge areas that define the cryo-EM experience
Sample
  • Size limitation — Particles <50 kDa challenging
  • Purity required — >90% homogeneity essential
  • Vitrification — Ice thickness, preferred orientation, AWI
  • Reproducibility — Manual blotting highly variable
Microscopy
  • Beam damage — Electrons destroy biological structure
  • Low dose — Only 20-60 e-/A2 — very noisy images
  • Imperfect imaging — CTF, aberrations, charging
  • Beam-induced motion — Sample moves during exposure
Data Processing
  • Complex pipeline — Motion correction to 3D refinement
  • Heterogeneity — Multiple conformations complicate analysis
  • Interpretation — Map quality varies; model bias risk
  • Computing — GPU clusters needed; TB-scale data
The Bottom Line
Sample preparation remains the major bottleneck, requiring substantial expertise at every step.
Section 1: Cryo-EM Overview
The Reality: Slow, Manual, Non-reproducible
Why cryo-EM remains challenging despite the resolution revolution
Sample
  • Slow — Weeks to months of iterative optimization
  • Manual — Blotting by hand, variable ice thickness
  • Non-reproducible — Same sample, different grids, different results
  • Grid dependent — Ice quality varies hole-to-hole
  • Flexibility — Conformational heterogeneity limits resolution
Microscopy
  • Slow — Days of data collection per dataset
  • Manual (mostly) — Grid screening still requires expert judgment
  • Grid dependent — Ice quality determines data quality
  • Noisy images — Low dose (20-60 e-/A2) = very low SNR
  • Artifacts — CTF, beam-induced motion, specimen charging
Data Processing
  • Slow — Hours to weeks (GPU-dependent)
  • Manual (mostly) — Multiple steps require expert decisions
  • Heterogeneity — Dynamic/continuous conformations
  • Wasted particles — Most picked particles discarded
  • Interpretation — Multiple possible models from same data
