Section 1: Cryo-EM Overview
Electron Microscopy & Biological Samples
Why do we need cryo-EM?

⚠ The Problem

  • Vacuum damage — Water evaporates instantly
  • Radiation damage — Electrons break bonds
  • Fixation artifacts — Alters native conformation
  • Low contrast — Light atoms scatter weakly
  • Charging — Non-conductive specimens
➔

✔ The Cryo Solution

  • Vitreous ice — Rapid cooling in liquid ethane
  • Native state — Solution-like conformation
  • Reduced damage — Imaging at -196°C
  • Low-dose imaging — 20-60 e⁻/Å²
  • No staining — Native hydrated state

Key Components of Modern Cryo-TEM
What makes modern cryo-TEM different
Autoloader
  • Maintains specimen at −180 °C (liquid nitrogen)
  • Autoloader cassette holds up to 12 grids
  • Preserves vacuum during grid exchange
  • Enables unattended multi-grid collection
Direct Electron Detector
Indirect (scintillator+CCD) vs Direct (CMOS)
DQE: Direct detector > Film > CCD
  • No scintillator — direct electron counting on CMOS
  • DQE >2× over film/CCD at all frequencies
  • Single electron counting mode maximizes SNR
  • Movie-mode enables motion correction
Energy Filter
  • Prism separates electrons by energy loss
  • Energy slit selects zero-loss electrons
  • Removes inelastic scattering — improves SNR
  • Essential for thick specimens (>50 nm)

A Structural Biologist's Dream
No purification. Just look into the cell.

The Dream
Structural and cell biology provide complementary, yet unconnected visions of the inner workings of cells — cryo-EM and cryo-ET, as a connecting bridge, hold great promise to create structural depictions of macromolecules inside the cell itself.
Why not just purify?
cryo-EM still requires purification — visualization outside of the natural context in which macromolecules operate. Cell biologists image cells but always far from the resolution at which chemistry can be understood.
How close are we?
✓
Large complexes 1.55 Å in situ
Ribosome, nuclear pore — atomic detail inside the real cell
~
Medium complexes <7 Å in situ
Subtomogram averaging reaches near-atomic resolution
○
Small / dynamic proteins coming
Resolution limits remain — but improving rapidly

    Where Does the Time & Money Go?
    Resource analysis of the single particle cryo-EM pipeline
    ⏱ Time Investment
    • Sample prep — protein production & optimization: days to weeks per iteration
    • Grid screening — iterative grid evaluation: day(s) per iteration
    • Data processing — motion correction → CTF → 3D: days to weeks
    Early-stage sample prep dominates total turnaround time
    💰 Direct Costs
    • Acquisition — hourly beam time dominates: $$$
    • Processing — GPU cluster compute at scale: $–$$
    • Sample prep — reagents and consumables: $$
    Microscope time is the single largest direct cost per dataset
    ⚡ What's Improving
    • Vitrification — Chameleon (~ms), VitroJet (~s): eliminates manual blotting
    • AI picking — crYOLO, TOPAZ: unsupervised, replaces manual selection
    • Live processing — on-the-fly CTF & motion correction during collection
    Automation is targeting the highest-impact bottlenecks first

    Section 1: Cryo-EM Overview
    Challenges and Limitations of Cryo-EM
    Three areas where cryo-EM still demands substantial expertise
    Sample Preparation
    • Slow — Production → grid prep → screening cycles repeat; retrograde steps common
    • Manual — Vitrification largely manual; requires technical skill to master
    • Non-reproducible — Same params → different ice quality grid-to-grid
    • AWI — Denaturation, preferred orientation, complex disassembly at the air-water interface
    • Size limitation — Proteins <150 kDa have low contrast, difficult to align
    Microscopy
    • Slow — 5k–10k micrographs per structure; days of collection per dataset
    • Manual (mostly) — Target selection, grid screening, and parameter setup need operator judgment
    • Grid dependent — Ice quality directly determines data quality
    • Beam effects — Low dose (30–60 e⁻/Å²) yields noisy images; beam also induces specimen motion
    • Aberrations — CTF, beam tilt, coma, spherical/chromatic aberration limit resolution
    • Cost — 300 keV: $4–10 M; weeks-long beam access queues
    Data Processing
    • Slow — Millions of particles; iterative 3D classification; GPU clusters needed
    • Manual (mostly) — 2D class selection subjective; parameter tuning at each step
    • Heterogeneity — Conformational and compositional variation complicate classification
    • Wasted particles — Valid particles with rare conformations often discarded alongside true junk during classification
    • Data management — TB-scale data per day; storage and transfer increasingly a bottleneck
    What's Improving
    • Blotting-free — Chameleon, VitroJet, cryoWriter among growing number of automated devices with real-time ice monitoring
    • Grids — Graphene supports and functionalized coatings (Ni-NTA, streptavidin) reduce AWI effects
    What's Improving
    • Cold FEG — 0.3 eV spread → better coherence, SNR
    • AFIS — ~40 exposures per stage move
    • Compact TEMs — 100–200 keV at $1–5 M
    • Automated screening — AI-assisted grid evaluation emerging (e.g. Smart EPU); full automation not yet achieved
    What's Improving
    • Live processing — CryoSPARC Live; on-the-fly CTF & motion correction during collection
    • AI across pipeline — Picking (crYOLO, TOPAZ), denoising (Blush), model building (ModelAngelo) now available in major packages
    • Heterogeneity analysis — 3DVA, cryoDRGN, DynaMight now resolve continuous conformational landscapes
