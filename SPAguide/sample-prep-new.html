Sample Preparation

From protein to vitrified grid

Goal of Sample Preparation

The Goal

  • Native state — Preserve biological structure without artifacts
  • Maximum contrast — Embed in thin vitreous ice for optimal imaging
  • Homogeneity — Uniform, monodisperse particles in solution
  • Reproducibility — Consistent grid quality across preparations

For High Resolution

  • Best specimen — Pure, stable, well-behaved protein
  • Optimal magnification — Balance pixel size vs. field of view
  • Best detector — High DQE for maximum signal capture
  • Best micrographs — Thin ice, no drift, proper defocus

➤ Leading to highest resolution structure with least data

Negative Staining vs. Cryo-EM

Negative staining

  • ✓ Faster & easier
  • ✓ Less expensive
  • ✓ Higher contrast / low resolution
  • ✓ Artifacts from the staining
  • ✓ Can be done at room temp.

Cryo-EM (Vitrification)

  • ✓ Slower & difficult
  • ✓ Expensive
  • ✓ Lower contrast / high resolution
  • ✓ Retain almost native state
Useful for QC of the sample (aggregation, dissociation, heterogeneity)
The SNR Challenge in Cryo-EM
Why we need thousands of particles

Why SNR is Low

  • Radiation sensitivity — Biological samples damaged by electrons
  • Low dose imaging — Only 20–60 e−/Å² per exposure
  • Weak scatterers — Light atoms (C, N, O, H) scatter weakly
  • Ice background — Vitreous ice adds noise

Solution

  • Average thousands to millions of particle images
  • Signal adds up, noise cancels out (√N improvement)
  • Requires homogeneous, well-distributed particles

Ordered Mass, Not Molecular Weight
What matters is the rigid, structured portion

Key Concept

  • Flexible regions average out → invisible in density
  • Only ordered, rigid portions contribute to the map
  • A 200 kDa complex with flexible domains may behave like 80 kDa

Example: hSAGA

  • Large complex (~1.8 MDa total)
  • Multiple flexible modules
  • Only the core is well-resolved
Henderson 1995 — The Size Limit
Theoretical lower bound for single-particle cryo-EM

Henderson’s Analysis

  • Theoretical minimum: ~38 kDa at 3 Å resolution
  • Based on electron scattering cross-sections
  • Assumes perfect imaging conditions
  • Published in Q. Rev. Biophys. (1995)

Practical Reality

  • Real-world limit was much higher for decades
  • DED + software advances now pushing below 50 kDa

Ethane Contamination

  • Trapped ethane from plunging
  • Appears as dark spots or streaks
Air-Water Interface (AWI)

At the AWI, proteins can undergo denaturation, aggregation or preferred orientation

Negatively impacts the final image quality

Glaeser, Curr Opin Colloid Interface Sci (2018)
Spot-to-Plunge Time & AWI
Faster freezing = less AWI damage

Key Finding (Chameleon)

  • Particles can move 100 nm within <1 ms
  • Denaturation follows in as little as nanoseconds
  • Hemagglutinin: ~1 s, 500 ms, 100 ms
  • Insulin receptor: 600 ms, 200 ms

Implications

  • Longer blotting time → more AWI damage
  • Traditional blotting (~3–5 s) allows extensive damage
  • Spray/jet devices minimize exposure time
Noble et al., Nat Methods (2018)
Continuous Support Films
Mitigating AWI effects with thin supports

Types

  • Continuous carbon — Oldest solution, adds background noise
  • Graphene — Single atom thick, minimal noise
  • Graphene oxide — Easier to make, slightly more noise

Benefits

  • Particles adhere to support instead of AWI
  • Reduces preferred orientation
  • Enables imaging of small proteins
  • More uniform particle distribution
Grid Optimization Strategies

When Standard Prep Fails

  • Preferred orientation → Tilted data collection, support films, detergents
  • AWI denaturation → Faster freezing, surfactants, support films
  • Poor ice → Different grid types, adjusted blot parameters
  • Aggregation → Buffer optimization, cross-linking (GraFix)
Kampjut et al., iScience (2021)
