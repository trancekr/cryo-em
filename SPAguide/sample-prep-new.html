Sample Preparation

From protein to vitrified grid

Goal of Sample Preparation

The Goal

  • Native state — Preserve biological structure without artifacts
  • Maximum contrast — Embed in thin vitreous ice for optimal imaging
  • Homogeneity — Uniform, monodisperse particles in solution
  • Reproducibility — Consistent grid quality across preparations

For High Resolution

  • Best specimen — Pure, stable, well-behaved protein
  • Optimal magnification — Balance pixel size vs. field of view
  • Best detector — High DQE for maximum signal capture
  • Best micrographs — Thin ice, no drift, proper defocus
  • Ultimate goal — Highest resolution structure with least data
The Circle of Cryo-EM Sample Preparation
An iterative process of optimization

Key Steps

  • Protein expression & purification
  • Quality assessment (SEC, DLS, mass photometry)
  • Grid preparation (glow discharge, sample application, blotting)
  • Vitrification (plunge freezing)
  • Grid screening (atlas → square → hole view)

Reality

  • Multiple rounds of optimization are typical
  • Each variable affects the final result
  • 3-year facility data shows iterative nature

Negative Staining vs. Vitrification

Negative Staining

  • Faster & easier preparation
  • Higher contrast, lower resolution (~15–25 Å)
  • Staining artifacts (flattening, dehydration)
  • Excellent for QC: check aggregation, heterogeneity

Cryo-EM (Vitrification)

  • Near-native state preservation
  • Lower contrast, higher resolution (<3 Å)
  • Requires thin vitreous ice
  • More demanding: time, equipment, expertise
The SNR Challenge in Cryo-EM
Why we need thousands of particles

Why SNR is Low

  • Radiation sensitivity — Biological samples damaged by electrons
  • Low dose imaging — Only 20–60 e−/Å² per exposure
  • Weak scatterers — Light atoms (C, N, O, H) scatter weakly
  • Ice background — Vitreous ice adds noise

Solution

  • Average thousands to millions of particle images
  • Signal adds up, noise cancels out (√N improvement)
  • Requires homogeneous, well-distributed particles
Wentinck et al., Curr Res Struct Biol (2022)

Augmentation Strategies

  • Fab/Nanobody — Add mass as fiducial marker
  • Megabody — Rigid scaffold increases ordered mass
  • Symmetry — Natural oligomers give more signal
  • Scaffold proteins — Fuse to large, rigid partner

Ordered Mass, Not Molecular Weight
What matters is the rigid, structured portion

Key Concept

  • Flexible regions average out → invisible in density
  • Only ordered, rigid portions contribute to the map
  • A 200 kDa complex with flexible domains may behave like 80 kDa

Example: hSAGA

  • Large complex (~1.8 MDa total)
  • Multiple flexible modules
  • Only the core is well-resolved
Henderson 1995 — The Size Limit
Theoretical lower bound for single-particle cryo-EM

Henderson’s Analysis

  • Theoretical minimum: ~38 kDa at 3 Å resolution
  • Based on electron scattering cross-sections
  • Assumes perfect imaging conditions
  • Published in Q. Rev. Biophys. (1995)

Practical Reality

  • Real-world limit was much higher for decades
  • DED + software advances now pushing below 50 kDa
Henderson, Q Rev Biophys (1995)

Anatomy of a Cryo-EM Grid

Grid Base

  • Material: Cu, Au, Ni, Ti (3 mm diameter)
  • Mesh: 200, 300, 400, or hexagonal
  • Gold grids reduce beam-induced motion

Foil

  • Material: Amorphous carbon, gold (UltrAuFoil)
  • Type: Continuous or holey
  • Hole size: 1.2/1.3, 2/1, 0.6/1 µm

Support Film (Optional)

  • Amorphous carbon, graphene, graphene oxide
  • Helps with preferred orientation & small proteins
Grid Handling
Careful handling is essential — grids are fragile
Vitrification — Plunge Freezing
Converting liquid water to vitreous ice in milliseconds

Steps

  • 1. Glow discharge (hydrophilize)
  • 2. Apply sample (2–4 µL)
  • 3. Blot excess liquid
  • 4. Plunge into liquid ethane

Critical Variables

  • Blot time & force
  • Humidity (>90%)
  • Temperature (4°C typical)

Hole View — Other Ice Issues

Ice Thickness Gradient

  • Thicker at edges, thinner at center
  • Particles concentrate at edges (meniscus effect)

Ethane Contamination

  • Trapped ethane from plunging
  • Appears as dark spots or streaks
The Air-Water Interface Problem
The #1 enemy of cryo-EM sample preparation

What Happens at the AWI

  • Adsorption — Proteins migrate to the interface within milliseconds
  • Denaturation — Hydrophobic regions unfold at the interface
  • Preferred orientation — Particles adopt limited viewing angles
  • Aggregation — Concentrated at thin film surfaces

Ice Film Dimensions

  • Total thickness: ~100–150 nm
  • Particle diameter: ~10–15 nm
  • Two AWIs sandwich the thin film
Spot-to-Plunge Time & AWI
Faster freezing = less AWI damage

Key Finding

  • Particles reach AWI in <1 ms
  • Denaturation begins in nanoseconds
  • Faster freezing preserves more native structure

Implications

  • Traditional blotting (~3–5 s) allows extensive AWI damage
  • Spray/jet devices minimize exposure time
Noble et al., Nat Methods (2018)
Continuous Support Films
Mitigating AWI effects with thin supports

Types

  • Continuous carbon — Oldest solution, adds background noise
  • Graphene — Single atom thick, minimal noise
  • Graphene oxide — Easier to make, slightly more noise

Benefits

  • Particles adhere to support instead of AWI
  • Reduces preferred orientation
  • Enables imaging of small proteins
  • More uniform particle distribution
Grid Optimization Strategies

When Standard Prep Fails

  • Preferred orientation → Tilted data collection, support films, detergents
  • AWI denaturation → Faster freezing, surfactants, support films
  • Poor ice → Different grid types, adjusted blot parameters
  • Aggregation → Buffer optimization, cross-linking (GraFix)
Drulyte et al., iScience (2021)
Sample Requirements Summary

Size & Mass

  • >80 kDa of ordered mass (routine)
  • 40–80 kDa possible with optimization
  • <40 kDa requires augmentation strategies

Quantity & Concentration

  • 0.5–1 mg protein per session
  • 50 nM – 5 µM concentration
  • Buffer: avoid glycerol, sucrose, high salt

Quality

  • >90% purity (SDS-PAGE)
  • Monodisperse (SEC, DLS)
  • Stable at 4°C during preparation
  • No aggregation tendency
The Bottom Line
Good sample = good data. No amount of microscope time or data processing can fix a bad sample.
